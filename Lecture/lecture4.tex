\subsection{Analysis of Divide-and-Conquer Algorithms}

The running time $T(n)$ of a divide-and-conquer algorithm is described by a
\textbf{recurrence} of the form:
\[
  T(n) =
  \begin{cases}
    \Theta(1)               & \text{if } n \le c, \\
    a\,T(n/b) + D(n) + C(n) & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
\]
where $D(n)$ is the cost of division and $C(n)$ is the cost of combination. For merge
sort, this gives $T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n)$, which results in $T(n) = \Theta(n\log n)$.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Solving Recurrences}

There are three main methods.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Substitution Method}

We \textbf{guess} the form of the solution, then use mathematical induction
to prove the constants.

\paragraph{Example: $T(n) = T(n/4 + 1) + T(3n/4 - 1) + \Theta(1)$.}
\emph{Let us show that $T(n) = O(n)$, i.e., $T(n) \le cn - d$ for constants $c, d > 0$.}

\begin{proof}
By inductive hypothesis, the recursive calls satisfy the bound. With $\Theta(1) \le k$:
\begin{align*}
  T(n) &\le c\!\left(\tfrac{n}{4}+1\right) - d
          + c\!\left(\tfrac{3n}{4}-1\right) - d + k \\
       &= cn - 2d + k.
\end{align*}
It suffices to choose $d \ge k$ to have $T(n) \le cn - d$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Master Theorem}

A "cookbook" for recurrences of the form $T(n) = aT(n/b) + f(n)$
(with $a \ge 1$, $b > 1$). We compare $f(n)$ and $n^{\log_b a}$:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
\begin{tabular}{clll}
\toprule
\textbf{Case} & \textbf{Condition on $f(n)$} & \textbf{Result} & \textbf{Intuition} \\
\midrule
1 & $f(n) = O\!\left(n^{\log_b a - \varepsilon}\right)$, $\varepsilon>0$
  & $T(n) = \Theta\!\left(n^{\log_b a}\right)$
  & Leaves dominate \\
2 & $f(n) = \Theta\!\left(n^{\log_b a}\right)$
  & $T(n) = \Theta\!\left(n^{\log_b a}\log n\right)$
  & Cost is evenly distributed \\
3 & $f(n) = \Omega\!\left(n^{\log_b a + \varepsilon}\right)$, $\varepsilon>0$
  & $T(n) = \Theta(f(n))$
  & Root dominates \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Examples of application:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Merge Sort: $T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n)$.
        Here $a=2$, $b=2$, $n^{\log_2 2} = n$. Case 2 $\Rightarrow T(n)=\Theta(n\log n)$.
  \item Binary Search: $T(n) = T(n/2) + \Theta(1)$.
        Here $n^{\log_2 1} = 1$. Case 2 $\Rightarrow T(n)=\Theta(\log n)$.
  \item Strassen: $T(n) = 7T(n/2) + \Theta(n^2)$.
        $n^{\log_2 7} \approx n^{2.81} \succ n^2$. Case 1 $\Rightarrow T(n)=\Theta(n^{\log_2 7})$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Maximum Subarray Problem}

\textbf{Input:} an array $A[low \ldots high]$ of $n$ integers (some negative).\\
\textbf{Output:} the indices $i, j$ and the maximum sum $\sum_{k=i}^{j} A[k]$.

\subsubsection{Divide-and-Conquer Approach}

We divide $A$ into two halves $A[low \ldots mid]$ and $A[mid{+}1 \ldots high]$.
The maximum subarray is either:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Entirely in the \textbf{left} half,
  \item Entirely in the \textbf{right} half,
  \item \textbf{Crossing the midpoint} (partly in the left of $mid$, partly in the right).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\small]
  \draw[fill=blue!8] (0,0) rectangle (4,0.7);
  \draw[fill=orange!20] (4,0) rectangle (8,0.7);
  \draw[dashed, thick] (4,-0.2) -- (4,0.9);
  \node at (2, 0.35) {left};
  \node at (6, 0.35) {right};
  \node[below] at (4, -0.25) {$mid$};
  % Crossing subarray highlight
  \draw[keyred, very thick] (2.5, 0) rectangle (5.8, 0.7);
  \node[keyred, above] at (4.15, 0.75) {\small crossing};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The \textsc{Find-Max-Crossing-Subarray} function scans from $mid$ toward the left then
toward the right in $\Theta(n)$. The global recurrence is:
\[
  T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n) \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{T(n) = \Theta(n \log n)}
\]
